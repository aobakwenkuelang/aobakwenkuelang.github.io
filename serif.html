Serif vs Sans Serif Type

One of the many ways we can classify typography is whether or not the letterforms contain serifs. Serifs are decorative elements added to letters at the end or beginning of strokes. Serif typefaces are those with serifs while sans serif typefaces are those without serifs.

Sans serif (left) vs serif (right) type.

Serif Typefaces

Serifs are ideal when producing larger bodies of text as they can aid with readability and reading speed. This is especially true for large passages like those present in print eg books, newspapers, etc. Serifs are becoming less popular as more of what we read becomes digital (serifs do not work as well on digital screens), because of this they can be interpreted to signify tradition or orderliness.

Sans Serif Typefaces

Because of their compact nature, sans serif typefaces work best when space is at a premium. For example, smaller or lower resolution screens like those present on smartphones often limit legibility with the amount of text you may fit at any one point. Similarly, sans serif type works best when trying to communicate one message without any additional body copy for example on signage like that present in airports, highways or maps.
